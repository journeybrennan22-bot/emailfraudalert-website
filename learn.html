Brand & Government Impersonation

The brand you trust, the sender you shouldn't.

Scammers know you trust certain companies and government agencies. So they write emails that mention DocuSign, PayPal, Microsoft, the IRS, or your state DMV — complete with logos and official-sounding language. But the actual sender has nothing to do with that organization.

Our detection covers 100+ brands and government agencies across 13 categories — the most-targeted organizations globally, catching an estimated 87-90% of brand-based phishing attacks.
How Scammers Use It

You get an email saying "Your DocuSign document is ready for signature." It looks real. The formatting matches what you've seen before. But the sender address is notifications@secure-doc-center.com, not docusign.com. You click, enter your credentials, and now they have access to your accounts — or worse, you wire money based on fraudulent instructions in the "document."

Or you receive a notice that your "driver's license renewal requires immediate action" from what looks like your state DMV. The sender? A random domain with no .gov address in sight.

What We Scan For

When an email mentions a known brand or government agency, we check if the sender domain actually belongs to that organization. DocuSign only sends from @docusign.com and @docusign.net. PayPal only sends from @paypal.com. The IRS only sends from @irs.gov. Your state DMV only sends from .gov addresses.

If the email talks about one organization but comes from somewhere else, you'll see a warning.

2

Lookalike Email Addresses

One character. Total impersonation.

Criminals create email addresses nearly identical to someone you trust — your bank, your boss, your escrow officer, your client. They change one letter, add an extra character, or swap an "l" for a "1." You glance at it, it looks right, so you trust it.

How Scammers Use It

An escrow officer gets an email from "the seller" asking to change the wiring instructions for their proceeds. The email address is off by one character — sara.milller@gmail.com instead of sara.miller@gmail.com. Who checks for three L's? The officer wires $380,000 to a criminal's account. Within hours, the money hops through a chain of banks and lands overseas. It's gone.

What Makes Our Detection Different

Most security tools compare against a generic database. We compare incoming emails against YOUR actual contacts. If you've emailed sara.miller@gmail.com before and suddenly get an email from sara.milller@gmail.com, we flag it immediately. The closer the match, the more likely it's an impersonation attempt.

Examples
support@chase.com → support@chasse.com
john@firstam.com → john@firstarn.com
sara.miller@gmail.com → sara.milller@gmail.com

3

Reply-To Hijacking

Your reply goes to them, not who you think.

The "From" address looks legitimate — maybe even your CEO's real email. But hidden in the email header is a different "Reply-To" address. When you hit reply, your response goes straight to the scammer, not the person you think you're emailing.

How Scammers Use It

You get an email that appears to be from your boss asking you to process a vendor payment. You reply with questions, they answer convincingly, and you send the wire. You had a whole email conversation. But every single reply went to a criminal in another country who was pretending to be your boss.

Why This Is Dangerous

This attack is completely invisible in normal email use. The "From" line shows your boss's name and email. You have to manually inspect the email headers to see the reply-to mismatch — and nobody does that.

How It Works

From: ceo@yourcompany.com

Reply-To: ceo-yourcompany@gmail.com

You think you're emailing your CEO. You're emailing a criminal who now has whatever you sent them.

4

Invisible Character Tricks

Identical to your eyes. Different to computers.

The Cyrillic letter "а" looks exactly like the English letter "a" — but to a computer, they're completely different characters. Criminals swap these lookalike letters to create email addresses that are visually perfect forgeries. You literally cannot see the difference.

How Scammers Use It

You receive an email from "accounts@microsoft.com" — except that "о" in microsoft is Cyrillic, not English. Your eyes see Microsoft. The email looks legitimate. You reset your password through their fake link, and they now control your account.

Why Only Software Can Catch This

Human eyes cannot distinguish between these characters. They look identical. Only software scanning the actual character codes can detect the substitution.

Can You Tell the Difference?
microsoft.com (Latin letters)
micrоsoft.com (Cyrillic 'о')

Your eyes cannot detect this. Only software can.

5

Display Name Spoofing

The name you trust, the address you ignore.

Email programs show the sender's display name prominently — "Bank of America Security" or "IT Support" or "DocuSign" — while hiding or minimizing the actual email address. Criminals set any display name they want, regardless of what address they're actually sending from.

How Scammers Use It

Your inbox shows an email from "Chase Fraud Department." You open it, concerned about your account. But the actual sending address is xr7829@randomserver.net. You click the link, enter your banking login, and they drain your account.

What We Scan For

We flag emails where the display name contains trusted company keywords or official-sounding phrases but the actual email domain doesn't match. If someone calls themselves "PayPal Support" but emails from a Gmail address, that's a red flag.

What You See vs. Reality

In your inbox: "Bank of America Security"

Actual address: xr77281@randomdomain.com

In your inbox: "DocuSign"

Actual address: notreal@spamserver.net

Dangerous Keywords

The language of fraud.

Scam emails use specific phrases designed to pressure you into acting before you think. They create urgency, threaten consequences, impersonate authority figures, or request sensitive financial actions. These aren't random — they're proven manipulation tactics.

How Scammers Use It

You receive an email saying "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours" or "Updated wire instructions — please confirm receipt." The urgency makes you act fast. You send money or credentials without verifying, and it's gone.

7

Deceptive Domains

Familiar names, hidden geography.

Criminals register domains that look like trusted .com addresses by using country-code extensions that include ".com" in them. At a glance, paypal.com.br looks like it might be related to paypal.com. It's not — it's a completely separate domain that anyone can register.

They also use commercial domains designed to look like real country codes. Extensions like .uk.com, .us.com, and .de.com aren't official government domains — they're sold by private registrars to anyone willing to pay. But they look close enough to fool most people.

How Scammers Use It

You get an email from billing@amazon.com.co asking you to verify your payment method. You see "amazon.com" and your brain fills in the rest. But .com.co is a completely different domain, and anyone can register one. The real Amazon has nothing to do with this email.

Deceptive Patterns We Flag
amazon.com.co — Not Amazon
paypal.com.br — Not PayPal
apple.com.mx — Not Apple
irs.gov.tax-refund.com — Not the IRS
Fake Country-Lookalike Domains We Flag
.uk.com — Not a real UK domain
.us.com — Not a real US domain
.de.com, .eu.com, .au.com, .cn.com, .jp.com, .ru.com, .sa.com, .za.com

These are commercial domains sold by private registrars. They have nothing to do with the countries they reference. Anyone in the world can register one.

9

Suspicious Domains

The domains scammers love.

Not all domain extensions are created equal. Some top-level domains (TLDs) are heavily exploited by criminals because they're free to register, require no identity verification, or cost so little that scammers can register thousands at once and burn them after a single phishing campaign.

Why We Flag These

Spamhaus, a global threat intelligence organization, publishes a regularly updated list of the most abused TLDs on the internet. Symantec (now part of Broadcom) tracks similar data in their annual Internet Security Threat Report, ranking TLDs by the ratio of malicious domains to legitimate ones. The TLDs we flag consistently appear at the top of both lists.

A legitimate business is extremely unlikely to contact you from one of these domains. If your "bank" is emailing you from one of these addresses, something is very wrong.

High-Risk Domains We Flag
.tk, .ml, .ga, .cf — Free registration, no ID required. Massively abused because scammers can register unlimited domains at zero cost.
.cc — Cheap registration with minimal oversight. Popular with scammers who need domains that look slightly more credible than free ones.
.biz, .info, .shop, .club, .buzz, .icu — Low-cost generic TLDs that appear disproportionately in phishing and spam campaigns. Australia's parliament blocked .info domains entirely in 2011 due to elevated malware rates.

These domains aren't inherently malicious, and legitimate websites do exist on them. But when an email from one of these domains is asking you to click a link, verify your account, or send money, it deserves extra scrutiny.

Suspicious Routing

The hidden path your email took.

Sometimes an email shows one sender address but actually traveled through completely different servers. Email programs show this as "via" — like "john@company.com via sketchy-server.net." This can be legitimate (companies use email services like SendGrid or Mailchimp), but it can also indicate spoofing.

How Scammers Use It

An attacker sends an email that appears to be from your colleague but routes it through a random server they control. The "from" address looks right, but the email's path tells a different story.

What We Do

We maintain a list of trusted email routing services. When an email is routed through a server NOT on our whitelist — especially something random or suspicious — we flag it.

Legitimate Routing Services (No Warning)

SendGrid, Mailchimp, Constant Contact, Amazon SES, Google, Microsoft Office 365, Salesforce, HubSpot, and other major email infrastructure providers.

11

Gibberish Domains

Real companies don't email from keyboard smashes.

Scammers burn through domains fast. They register a random string, blast out thousands of phishing emails, and abandon it before anyone catches on. The result: emails from domains that look like someone fell asleep on the keyboard. No real business operates from xk7rm9.com or ndsm4.fr or vUqC@evda.hoyh.systctlpro.uk.com.

How Scammers Use It

You get an email about a "payment confirmation" or "account verification." The display name looks official. The logo looks right. But the actual sender address is something like alert4644@ndsm4.fr or notifications@xr7tk2q.shop. You'd catch it if you looked closely — but most people don't look at sender addresses at all.

What We Scan For

Our gibberish detection analyzes domain names for patterns that don't appear in legitimate business domains. We look for domains with no vowels (like "ndsm4" or "xkrtg"), unusually high ratios of numbers to letters, and chains of random-looking subdomains stacked together. A domain that fails multiple checks gets flagged.

Legitimate short abbreviations like msn.com, bbc.co.uk, and npm.org are excluded — we only flag patterns that are statistically unlikely to be real businesses.

Examples
alert4644@ndsm4.fr — No vowels, random string
vUqC@evda.hoyh.systctlpro.uk.com — Stacked gibberish subdomains
info@xr7tk2q9.shop — High digit ratio, suspicious TLD

12

Mass-Distributed Emails

Your bank doesn't CC 30 strangers on your account alert.

Legitimate invoices, payment confirmations, and account alerts are sent to one person — you. When an email about "your" account or "your" payment is blasted to 10, 20, or 50 recipients at once, that's a mass phishing campaign, not a personal notification.

How Scammers Use It

You receive an email saying "Your payment of $459.99 has been processed." It looks like a receipt from a company you use. But look at the To and CC fields — the same email was sent to dozens of people. The scammer is casting a wide net, hoping a few recipients will panic and click the "dispute this charge" link.

What We Scan For

We count the total recipients in the To and CC fields. When an email is sent to 10 or more people and contains content that should be personal — payment confirmations, account alerts, invoice notifications — we flag it. Real companies sending bulk email use BCC or marketing platforms. Scammers just dump addresses into the To field.

Why It Works

Most people never look at who else received the email. They see their name, they see an amount, they panic. The mass distribution is hiding in plain sight — you just have to know to look for it.
