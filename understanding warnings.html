Email Fraud Alert Isn't Perfect

We'd rather warn you once too often than miss the one that matters. Here's why that tradeoff exists and how to use our warnings effectively.

We're a Speed Bump, Not a Wall

Email Fraud Alert doesn't block emails or prevent you from doing anything. When we detect something suspicious, we show you a warning. You decide what to do next. Our job is to create a pause at the moment it matters — before you click, reply, or send money.

Why False Positives Happen

Scammers are effective because they sound exactly like legitimate senders. When a criminal impersonates the IRS, they use the same words the real IRS would use. When they pose as your bank, they write emails that look identical to real bank emails. That's the whole point.

Email Fraud Alert detects patterns — specific phrases, domain structures, sender behaviors — that appear in fraud. But we can't see your relationships. We don't know that Janet is your actual accountant, or that you're expecting a document from your bank, or that Tiffany is your coworker's real name. We can only flag that something matches a pattern criminals use and let you decide.

We deliberately err on the side of caution. A false positive costs you two seconds of thought. A missed fraud costs $83,000 on average — and sometimes everything. Given that tradeoff, we'd rather show you one extra warning than miss the one that matters.

Every false positive we identify helps us improve. We're constantly tuning detection to reduce noise while catching real threats. If you see a warning on an email you're certain is legitimate, that's useful feedback — it helps us get smarter.

Common Situations That Trigger Warnings

Here are scenarios where you might see a warning on a legitimate email:

Your CPA mentions taxes

Emails discussing tax returns, refunds, or IRS forms may trigger warnings because scammers use the same language. If you know and trust the sender, the email is probably fine.

A company uses multiple domains

Large companies often send from different subdomains (support@mail.company.com vs. notifications@company.com). This can trigger mismatch warnings even when both are legitimate.

Document signing services

Platforms like DocuSign, Adobe Sign, or bank signing tools sometimes include footer text that resembles phishing tactics ("copy and paste this link"). If you're expecting a document to sign, verify directly with the sender.

Someone's name matches a brand

A coworker named Tiffany or Chase might trigger brand impersonation warnings. If you know the person, you can trust the email.

Marketing emails from trusted companies

Newsletters routed through email service providers (Mailchimp, SendGrid) may trigger routing warnings. If you subscribed to the list, you're fine.

When You See a Warning, Ask Yourself:

Three questions that matter

  1. Was I expecting this email?
  2. Do I know and trust the sender?
  3. Is this asking me to click a link, send money, or share sensitive information?

If something feels off — even a little — pick up the phone and verify. Call the person or company directly using a number you already have, not one from the email. That two-second pause is the whole point of Email Fraud Alert.

Why We Err on the Side of Caution

A $150,000 wire fraud starts with a single email that looked legitimate. The sender name was familiar. The request seemed reasonable. The only sign something was wrong was buried in details most people never check.

We'd rather show you one extra warning on a real email than miss the one that matters. Our detection gets smarter every day, and we're constantly working to reduce unnecessary warnings while catching real threats.

The Bottom Line

Email Fraud Alert is a second pair of eyes, not a replacement for your judgment. When you see a warning, pause and think. When you're uncertain, verify. When something feels wrong, trust your instincts.
